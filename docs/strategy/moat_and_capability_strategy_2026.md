# Krafted For You Moat And Capability Strategy 2026

Date: `2026-05-24`

Scope: tactical plan to build the first two moat capabilities:

1. `Customer Emotion Intelligence`
2. `Curated Option Architecture`

## 1. Capability Goal

Krafted For You should learn to translate a vague customer intention into a refined, emotionally accurate, feasible gift proposal faster and better than generic bespoke artists.

The desired customer experience:

`I did not know exactly what to give, but Krafted For You understood the person, narrowed the choices, and gave me beautiful options that felt right.`

## 2. Success Definition By Strategy Layer

| Strategy layer | Success for Customer Emotion Intelligence | Success for Curated Option Architecture | Ways to achieve it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Business Strategy | The brand owns a clear emotional job in the customer's mind | Offer choices are bounded and premium, not infinite | Define target occasions, recipient types, value promises, and refusal rules |
| Operations Strategy | Every serious inquiry becomes a structured brief | Every qualified lead gets 2-3 clear option paths | Intake template, brief scoring, option templates, quote workflow |
| Marketing Strategy | Content shows gifts as emotional stories, not products alone | Marketing teaches customers what kinds of options exist | Brief-to-gift stories, customer use cases, option-path examples |
| Digital Strategy | Inquiry forms capture emotional and practical inputs | Digital touchpoints guide customers into offer lanes | Guided inquiry form, example gift paths, proof library |
| Agent Automation Strategy | Agents summarize customer intent and missing information | Agents draft option paths for human review | Prompt library, lead schema, option-generation rules, approval matrix |

## 3. Tactical Approaches

### Approach A: Founder-Led Manual System

Use a simple document or spreadsheet and apply the process manually for every serious inquiry.

Pros:

- fastest to start,
- teaches the founder what questions matter,
- avoids premature tool design.

Challenges:

- inconsistent usage when busy,
- founder may skip fields from memory,
- hard to analyze without discipline.

How to overcome:

- keep the first version short,
- make the brief one page,
- review only 5-10 inquiries first,
- update the template weekly.

### Approach B: Agent-Assisted Briefing

Paste customer messages or founder notes into an agent prompt. The agent extracts the structured brief, missing questions, emotional intent, and option paths.

Pros:

- reduces founder thinking time,
- creates structured memory,
- speeds quote drafting.

Challenges:

- agent may over-interpret vague data,
- emotional tone may become generic,
- output still needs taste review.

How to overcome:

- require confidence labels,
- mark assumptions clearly,
- keep all customer-facing replies as drafts,
- compare agent interpretation against founder judgment.

### Approach C: Guided Inquiry Form

Create a short inquiry form that captures practical and emotional inputs.

Pros:

- improves data quality,
- reduces back-and-forth,
- prepares future automation.

Challenges:

- forms can feel impersonal,
- too many questions reduce completion,
- customers may not know answers upfront.

How to overcome:

- use a warm introduction,
- ask only essential questions,
- allow "not sure yet",
- keep final consultation human.

### Approach D: Offer Playbooks

Create repeatable playbooks for top occasions and recipient types.

Pros:

- makes option generation faster,
- supports premium consistency,
- helps agents draft better proposals.

Challenges:

- risk of becoming generic,
- too many playbooks create maintenance burden.

How to overcome:

- start with 3 occasions,
- define emotional goal plus personalization levers,
- use playbooks as proposal scaffolds, not fixed products.

## 4. Data Sources

| Data source | Use for Customer Emotion Intelligence | Use for Curated Option Architecture |
|---|---|---|
| Past orders | Identify why customers bought and what emotions they wanted | Identify repeatable hamper/product structures |
| Customer chats | Extract exact customer language, hesitation, budget cues | Identify common choices, objections, missing information |
| Founder memory | Reconstruct stories behind best and worst orders | Identify what made orders easy or chaotic |
| Photos/videos | Map aesthetics, materials, product types, presentation styles | Build example options and proof assets |
| Testimonials/reactions | Learn what customers value after delivery | Find which gift concepts customers remember |
| Local market sourcing notes | Understand feasible materials and product categories | Build realistic option menus |
| Competitor examples | Identify overused category language and visual sameness | Avoid generic option structures |
| Workshop interactions | Learn what people enjoy making and gifting | Create workshop-to-gift option paths |

## 5. Required Analysis

Analyze the first dataset of `20-30` past or recent orders, even if reconstructed from memory.

For each order, capture:

- occasion,
- buyer relationship to recipient,
- recipient profile,
- desired feeling,
- budget,
- lead time,
- handmade element,
- sourced element,
- what made it successful,
- what made it hard,
- final customer reaction,
- reusable option pattern.

## 7. Progress And Feedback Loop

| Review cadence | What to check | Decision to make |
|---|---|---|
| After every inquiry | Was the brief complete? Were the options clear? | Add missing question or simplify template |
| Weekly | Which option paths converted or stalled? | Improve playbooks and proposal language |
| Monthly | Are customers experiencing less confusion and faster clarity? | Adjust offer boundaries, pricing cues, or intake |
| Quarterly | Are these capabilities improving conversion, AOV, and founder time? | Decide whether to build tools, scripts, or more playbooks |

## 8. Success Metrics

| Metric | First target |
|---|---:|
| Serious inquiries captured in structured brief | 80% |
| Qualified inquiries receiving 2-3 option paths | 80% |
